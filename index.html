Chapter One
Brand Visual DNA
The aesthetic identity of Falcon House Atelier is built on restraint, reverence, and the poetry of craft. Every visual decision must pass a single test: does this feel heirloom-worthy?
The Foundational Premise
We do not sell garments. We document heritage.

Every piece of content is an archival act. The camera is not a commercial tool — it is a witness to craftsmanship that took generations to perfect. This distinction determines every creative choice we make.

01
The Gaze — Stillness
Inspired by Sabyasachi's museum-quality frames. The camera does not move. The subject breathes but does not perform. Silence speaks. Every static frame must feel like it belongs in an archive — as though it was shot to be seen a hundred years from now.
02
The Drape — Movement
Drawn from Tarun Tahiliani's mastery of fabric in motion. When we show movement, it is purposeful — a hem catching wind, a dupatta suspended mid-fall. Movement is never hurried. It is ceremonial. The fabric leads; the body follows.
03
Color Philosophy
Our palette is drawn from Indian mineral pigments and natural dyes — muted terracotta, ivory, aged gold, deep jewel tones. We never use filters that bleach or over-saturate. The garment's color must be truthful. Post-production must preserve, not embellish.
04
Negative Space
Air is not absence — it is composition. Our frames breathe. The garment is never fighting for attention with backgrounds, props, or clutter. We design what is not shown as carefully as what is. White walls, raked light, stone floors — these are our backdrops.
Reference Principles
What we extract from the masters — not copy

From Sabyasachi: the weight of history, archival captions, craft reverence. From Tahiliani: fluidity, movement as storytelling, the modern drape. From Abu-Sandeep: opulence without vulgarity, layering as poetry. From Ritu Kumar: textile intelligence, the handwoven narrative. From Rhea Kapoor: contemporary curation, styling as editorial voice.

Chapter Two
Video Direction Guide
Every frame is a deliberate act. These are the cinematographic laws that govern all moving image content for Falcon House Atelier.
Shot Composition Rules
Macro / Extreme Close-Up
Thread-level detail. Used for embroidery, weave structure, and finishing. Camera at 2–5cm. Rack focus from background motif to foreground thread. Purpose: to show what the eye cannot see in a showroom. This is proof of craft, not marketing.
Close-Up — The Detail Frame
Hands on fabric. Jewelry against drape. Zari border at the hem. Camera angle: 30–45° from surface for maximum texture depth. Lighting: raking side-light only. No frontal flash. Duration: 4–8 seconds, locked frame, slight breathe permitted.
Mid-Shot — The Portrait Frame
Waist to crown. The garment as a complete statement. Model does not smile. Eyes: slightly above lens or angled away — never directly at camera for product shots. Expression: dignified neutrality. The garment is the protagonist; the wearer, the setting.
Wide / Environmental Frame
Full body in context: archway, garden, stone terrace, empty haveli corridor. Used sparingly. Purpose: to establish world, not sell product. The environment must be as curated as the garment. Rule: if the background requires explanation, it is wrong.
Tracking / Movement Shot
Camera moves with the subject at hip-height, tracking from front or side. Purpose: to capture how fabric behaves in motion. Speed: 40–60% of natural walking pace. The camera never rushes. No handheld shake. Stabilizer mandatory. The drape must be the visual event.
The First 3 Seconds — Hook Formulas
Non-Negotiable Law
No spoken word. No text overlay. No music drop. Only the garment speaks.

The first three seconds of every reel must create desire through image alone. Sound design, when used, is ambient — the rustle of silk, a silver payel's chime, the breath of a loom.

01
Texture Reveal
Begin in extreme close-up on fabric texture — so close it is abstract. Slowly pull back over 3 seconds until the garment becomes recognizable. The viewer's brain resolves the image with pleasure. Works for: Banarasi, Chikankari, heavy embroidery.
02
Light Strike
Begin in deliberate shadow or silhouette. A beam of natural light rakes across the embroidery — revealing Gota, Zardozi, or Sequin work in a single, breathtaking moment. No cuts. One continuous movement of light. Best shot at golden hour with a flag and a reflector.
03
Fabric Fall
Shot from above or at floor-level: fabric unfurling, a lehenga spreading, a dupatta released from hands. Slow motion: 120fps minimum, played at 50% speed. No music — only the whisper of silk. Begin the reel mid-fall. Let the audience arrive at the beginning of something beautiful.
04
Sound Identity
Begin on black. First: a single sound — the click of an antique clasp, a payel chime, the snap of a silk dupatta in wind. Then image. Builds brand audio identity over time. Every sound used must be real and recorded on set, never stock. This is the acoustic equivalent of our visual signature.
05
The Artisan's Hand
Extreme close-up of a craftsperson's hands at work — needle drawing zari, fingers pressing a block, a weaver threading a loom. Pull out slowly to reveal: the finished garment, worn. The journey revealed in reverse. This hook is the most emotionally resonant. Use for hero campaigns only.
Lighting Setups
Warm Heritage
Golden dusk. 3200K. Directional from 45° side. Single source, large diffusion. No fill. Deep amber shadows. Use for: silks, heavily embroidered pieces, heirloom storytelling.
Neutral Editorial
5500K studio white. Soft box from above, slight front fill. No shadows on face. Garment-accurate color reproduction. Use for: product detail shots, Chikankari, cotton, pastel lehengas.
Shadow Royal
Low-key. Single hard source from one side. Deep shadows consuming 60% of frame. High contrast. Reveals structure, not surface. Use for: dark silks, Benarasi brocade, menswear, nightwear campaigns.
Chapter Three
Caption Playbook
Language is craft. A caption is not a description — it is a door. It must open a world the image has already begun to build.
The Sabyasachi Reference
Historical · Poetic · Archival
Opens with a cultural or historical reference — a year, a city, a dynasty, a craft form.
Sentences are long, unhurried, weighted with detail.
Never uses exclamation marks. Ever.
Credits are precise: craftsperson's name, cluster, technique.
Reads like a museum label written by a poet.
The Rhea Kapoor Reference
Contemporary · Styling-Led · Confident
Opens in the present tense — what is happening now, how this was worn, why.
Short sentences. Declarative. No hedging.
Styling decisions are explained, not apologized for.
Luxury casualness — the tone of someone who knows the craft and wears it easily.
Reads like a conversation with the world's most stylish friend.

The [technique] you see here — each [motif] requiring [time] of uninterrupted work — is not simply pattern. It is an argument for the irreplaceable value of human hands. This [garment name] is the result of [number] hours of labor, and it belongs to the woman who understands what she is choosing to carry forward.

We chose [color] for its relationship to [cultural or seasonal reference]. It exists in [quantity] pieces. Each signed.
Template 03 — Styling Masterclass How we styled the [garment name]: paired with [jewellery description] from [source if relevant], [footwear], [blouse/inner detail]. The [dupatta/stole] is draped [specific drape method]. We kept hair [style] because the [garment detail — e.g. neckline, shoulder work] asks to be seen without interruption. Formatting Laws
Caption formatting is non-negotiable

Line breaks between thought-paragraphs. No emoji in caption body — one optional emoji in first comment only, never in the caption. Credits on a new line, separated by a line break from the caption. Use em dash (—) not hyphen (-). No exclamation marks. No hashtags in caption — hashtags in first comment, maximum 15, all lowercase. Price is never mentioned in caption — direct to link or DM.

Chapter Four
Textile Shooting Guide
Each textile has its own visual logic. Shooting a Chikankari like a Gota Patti piece is a failure of intelligence. This guide governs fabric-specific cinematographic decisions.
Chikankari
  • Shoot in diffused natural light — never harsh direct sun
  • Side-rake light at 20° reveals the shadow-depth of the pull-work
  • Background: ivory, stone, or pale sage — never white
  • The value of Chikankari is in its shadow, not its thread
  • Macro shot: isolate a single motif — buteh, jali, or phanda
  • In motion: shoot against backlight for translucency effect
  • Camera: f/2.8 or wider for extreme bokeh on pull-work depth
Gota Patti
  • Shoot in warm directional light — the gold must gleam
  • Camera at 30–45° to surface to capture shimmer angle
  • Never shoot Gota in overhead light — it goes flat and dead
  • Move the garment slightly during close-up — the light must dance
  • Background: dark velvet, aged wood, or deep terracotta
  • Best shot at golden hour — the metal thread reads as alive
  • Avoid flash entirely — it bleaches the gold to silver
Silk — Banarasi / Kanjivaram
  • Shoot in movement — silk's value is in its fall and sheen
  • 120fps for drape shots; the weave should be visible in slow-mo
  • Studio white light for true color — silks lie in golden light
  • For brocade: raking side-light at 45° reveals the weave relief
  • Background: neutral grey, white plaster, or natural stone
  • Never let silk pool on the floor unless styled intentionally
  • Macro on the zari border for campaign hero shots
Cotton — Mul / Jamdani
  • Shoot outdoors in open shade — cotton needs living light
  • Emphasize the texture: these fabrics should look tactile
  • Avoid over-styling — cotton is democratic luxury, keep it clean
  • For Jamdani: backlight reveals the woven motif in relief
  • Movement shots: wind or a hand-held fan creates beautiful motion
  • Do not over-press cotton before shooting — natural creases read as honest
  • Background: earth, terracotta, aged brick, greenery
Zardozi / Heavy Embroidery
  • This is a still-photography textile — avoid motion shots
  • Shoot on a flat surface (lay flat) for full-body embroidery shots
  • Macro reveals the three-dimensional relief of the metal work
  • Single directional key light from one side only
  • Never use umbrella or soft-box — you need shadow to show depth
  • Shoot the same piece at three focal lengths: macro, close-up, mid
  • Styled: model seated, not standing — the weight must be implied
Block Print / Dabu
  • Shoot in full, even light — the pattern is the story
  • Show repeat: enough fabric in frame to see at least 3 repeat units
  • Lay-flat with props: the block itself, raw pigment, aged hands
  • Outdoors only — natural light shows the ink's true depth and variation
  • Macro on a single motif: imperfection is the proof of authenticity
  • Do not steam block prints before shooting — the slight irregularity is the craft
  • Background: the earth it was made in — indigo, ochre, red soil
Sensory Capture Principle
The camera must describe what the hand feels

Softness: achieved through diffused backlight and open aperture, creating the impression of translucency and gentleness in cotton and georgette. Weight: achieved through slow motion, showing how a heavy lehenga settles — the delay between movement and stillness communicates mass. Transparency: shoot chiffon and organza against a strong backlight source, letting the weave itself become the subject. Craftsmanship density: show the garment from three distances in the same sequence — world, body, thread.

Chapter Five
Content Pillar System
Five pillars govern all content output. Every piece of content belongs to one pillar. Every pillar serves a distinct business and brand purpose. Balance across pillars is mandatory — no pillar dominates for more than two consecutive posts.
I
Campaign Reveal
High-production brand-building content. Full crew. Art-directed location. Professional model or muse. This is our museum-quality content — it must withstand time. Shot quarterly for major collections, monthly for smaller reveals. Every campaign reveal is a 60–90 second reel + 6–8 static frames. Never produced in haste.
Brand Building · High Production · Quarterly Anchor
II
Artisan Spotlight
Human and craft storytelling. We visit the karigars, the weavers, the block-printers. We show their hands, their process, their names. We never show their face without their consent. We caption with precision — name, cluster, years of practice. This content builds credibility that no campaign can manufacture. It is the proof of our promises.
Trust Building · Community · Raw Production
III
Styling Masterclass
Utility-driven content that teaches the customer how to wear what we make. This is not a lookbook — it is an education. How to drape a dupatta five ways. How to layer a kurta for winter. How to style the same lehenga for day and evening. This content creates attachment and reduces purchase anxiety. The voice is confident and generous.
Engagement · Education · Conversion Support
IV
Product Focus
Sales-oriented content that shows the garment at its best from multiple angles. This is not commercial content — it is considered editorial with a clear product at the center. Garment name, fabric, technique, and enquiry information in caption. Static frame + one 15-second detail reel per product. Clean. Decisive. Respectful of the customer's time.
Revenue · Catalogue · Conversion
V
Texture Reel
Detail-focused, short-form content (15–30 seconds). Close-up of a single textile detail. No model required. No location required. Produced in a studio with a single, well-considered light source. This pillar fills the weekly calendar without diluting brand equity. It is the everyday voice — quiet, obsessive, craft-focused. Music: ambient or natural sound only.
Aesthetic Consistency · Filler · Craft Pride

Chapter Seven
Execution Checklist
Tap each item to mark complete. These checklists govern pre-shoot, day-of, and post-production workflows.
Pre-Shoot — 72 Hours Before
Shot list finalized and approved by creative director
Garments pressed, steamed, and tagged by design code
Location scouted and light quality confirmed at shoot hour
Props curated: nothing purchased, only found or borrowed antiques
Model briefed on posture, gaze, and movement philosophy
Jewelry and accessories confirmed and matched to garments
Caption drafts prepared for all garments to be shot
On Set — Day Of Shoot
First frame test: check that no background element competes with garment
Lighting confirmed against reference mood board before model arrives
Sound recorded for ambient audio (fabric, jewelry, environment)
Each garment shot at minimum three focal lengths: macro, mid, full
Movement shot captured for every garment with drape quality
BTS documented without overexposing the craft process
Every image reviewed on external monitor — not camera screen
Post Production — Edit Lock
Color grade applied: warm, neutral, or shadow-royal per garment type
No skin-smoothing or liquifying applied — we show real people
Fabric color checked against physical garment under D65 monitor
Every reel: first 3 seconds reviewed for hook quality before export
Audio mixed: ambient sound at -18dB, no music clipping
Caption finalized, proofread, credits confirmed with full names
Hashtag bank selected from approved list — first comment only
Posting time confirmed per engagement data (not assumption)
Chapter Eight
The Luxury Rulebook
These are not guidelines. They are laws. Deviation from these rules is a deviation from who we are. The brand is protected by its constraints, not liberated from them.
The Do's — Falcon House Laws
Every frame must earn its existence — if it adds nothing, remove it
Show the craft at macro level at least once per product
Name the artisan when known — always
Let silence do the work that music might overclaim
Use negative space as an active compositional element
Write captions as if they will be read in a hundred years
Shoot the same garment multiple times — the best frame is rarely the first
Allow imperfection in handcraft to be visible — it is the proof
Restrict your palette to what the garment demands
Move slowly — in camera, in editing, in posting cadence
The Don'ts — Non-Negotiable
No cluttered visuals — backgrounds must never compete with garment
No aggressive selling language — not in captions, not in stories
No fast-fashion pacing — no rapid cuts, no trend-chasing audio
No filters that alter the truth of a textile's color
No exclamation marks — ever, in any context
No countdown timers, sale banners, or urgency tactics
No unplanned collaborations — every association reflects on us
No posting without a caption that has been written and reviewed
No skin retouching — we represent the women who wear us
No content that could have been made by anyone else
The Final Test — Apply to Every Frame Before Publishing
Could this frame exist in a museum fifty years from now?

Ask this of every image before it is posted. Not: is it pretty? Not: will it perform? Ask: is it worthy of the craft it is documenting? If the answer is yes — publish. If the answer is uncertain — return to the brief. The brand is built not in a single campaign, but in the accumulated weight of every frame we chose to show and every frame we had the discipline to withhold.

On Exclusivity
Mystery is the luxury brand's most valuable asset

We do not show everything. We do not explain everything. We do not answer every question in the caption. We create a space between what is shown and what is not — and the customer's imagination fills it with desire. A brand that over-shares loses its power. A brand that withholds thoughtfully becomes aspirational. Know the difference between secrecy and curation. We practice curation.
